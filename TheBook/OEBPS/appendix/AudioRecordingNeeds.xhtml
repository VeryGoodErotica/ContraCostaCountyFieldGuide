Audio Goals

A long-term goal of this project is to have quality audio recordings, loudness normalized to the EBU R128 Loudness standard, for as many bird, frog, and insect calls as possible.

For both quality and legal reasons, it really is my preference if these recordings are original to this project with the recordings made in either Contra Costa or Alameda Counties.

Both birds and frogs, and quite likely insects, have dialects. In fact these dialects often are a part of how speciation takes place.

When the dialects in different areas have diverged enough, they create a barrier to gene flow that results in the divergent populations heading down different evolutionary paths.

It is my strong desire for as many audio recordings as possible to have photo vouchers accompanying the recording to help validate the species that was recorded, along with geolocation data. This information should be vouchered in the iNaturalst with audio recordings also submitted to the Cornell Lab of Ornithology Macaulay Library.

A lot of taxonomy work in birds is taking place, and there certainly will be Cryptic Species Complexes discovered where variations in the song are one way to distinguish species within the complex.

With birds, an identifiable photo voucher of the actual bird being recorded can also verify whether the song is coming from a male or female of the species, there is often sexual dimorphism in the singing and it would be great to have songs from both sexes available in the species profiles.

Types of Audio Recordings

There are two basic types of audio recordings I want to have as part of this project:

Single Specimen Recordings
This is the recording of the sound made by a specific specimen to the exclusion of noise around it. These recordings are best done with a directional microphone aimed at the specimen to be recorded.
Chorus Recording
This is the recording of the sound made by group, often separated from each other enough that directional microphones do not work for this kind of recording.

Audio Mastering

For submissions to Macaulay Library, it probably is best for NO modifications of the PCM data to take place, for risk of damaging the waveform. The researchers that use the audio recordings should have the original data.

For use within this field guide, some mastering will take place, specifically the perceived loudness normalization so that all audios within the field guide have the same perceived loudness.

For audios recorded with a Parabolic Microphone, they are great for a directional recording at a distance but they distort the recording. The physics of how they work, higher frequencies are amplified more than lower frequencies.

Using a high-pass filter to remove frequencies below what it wanted first, it may be possible to use a digital filter to compensate for this frequency-based amplification distortion by digitally amplifying the lower frequencies above the high pass filter, similar to how the RIAA Equalization curve works. I still have to look into it.

Each audio should also have a clear voice with a short description of the species and other data (such as sex) prepended to the audio, to help avoid confusion.

For space reasons, after normalization the audios will be lossy encoded using the Ogg Opus container and codec at 64kbps. Opus is a mature IETF standard that has been well-supported by everyone except Apple for years. The problem with using AAC, unless you use HEv1 profile there is no guarantee an ePub player that supports audio will be able to properly decode it, often resulting in very subpar playback. With the HEv1 profile, you need a much higher bitrate to get the same quality you can get with Opus at 64kbps. File size then becomes a very real issue.

Hopefully in the not too distant future, Apple will get over itself and support Ogg Opus. They already support the Opus codec itself since iOS 11, but they still refuse to support the Ogg container needed for it to work with their ePub readers.

Audio mastering will be done with a combination of Audacity, SoX, FFmpeg (for EBU R128 normalization), and Opus Tools.

Those are high quality free software projects and do not have any cost involved. Well, other than my time.

Hardware Description

When nature is your recording studio, there are several different hardware components that work in conjunction with each other:

  • Digital Recording Device
  • Microphone & Battery Pack
  • Pistol Grip with Foam Wind Screen

Digital Recording Device

For quality recordings, which is the goal, the quality of the microphone is far more important than the quality of the digital recording device. The microphone takes the sound waves and converts them into an analog electrical current signal. It does not matter how good the ADC in the recording device is if that current signal is crap.

That being said, if the digital recording device is too low quality, the electrical signal it converts to bits will suffer greatly. Smart phones do not cut it, they degrade a quality input signal.

The recording device must be able to handle at least 48kHz/24 bit PCM data smoothly. 24 bits per sample gives more flexibility when setting the recording level, you can be a little conservative without suffering audible quantization noise when amplifying the recording later. If the recording level is too high, the recording clips and is useless, but if you record too low with 16 bits per sample, amplifying during mastering can result in audible quantization noise.

XLR for the microphone input is beneficial but not absolutely mandatory if the device has a quality 3.5mm microphone input jack. A cheap 3.5mm connector can create noise in the signal before the conversion to digital takes place.

The recording device must also have sufficient use time before that battery needs to be recharged, and should have replaceable batteries if they run low while out in the field.

For me, the size of the unit is also a consideration. I like to hike, I already carry camera equipment etc. and do not want a recording unit that is too large.

The recording device that meets all my needs is the Roland R-07. It lacks XLR support but I do not see that being a huge issue.

As a compact unit, this also would make a good backup unit to carry with me in the future if I ever do upgrade to a more expensive unit.

The Roland R-07 costs about $200.

Another option is the Tascam DR-100MkIII. It has the advantage of an XLR input and rates a little better in recording quality according to Macaulay Library tests, but the battery does not give as many hours of record time and it costs $100 more.

With respect to the better recording quality, that was measured by spectral analysis tests rather than human listening tests. Whether the difference is audible or not to human ears, that was not tested. Differences in spectral analysis differences are not always audible in double blind listening tests.

The battery life is a potential issue if used for Chorus Recording. I honestly think I would rather put the $100 difference into the microphone.

Microphone & Battery Pack

When the Digital Recording Device has an XLR input it actually can be used to power the microphone as well. With only a 3.5mm jack, the microphone needs its own power source. Even with an XLR input, I think I would prefer the microphone to have its own power source anyway.

I have no plans to use a parabolic microphone. They do have farther directional range than a shotgun microphone, but they require more skill in targeting them and are more difficult to pack for hiking due to the parabola dish.

For recording Chorus Recording, I actually plan to build my own, explained in a minute.

The microphone I need to start recording audios for this project is a shotgun microphone. There are two options I am considering:

Rode NTG2

This shotgun microphone is the cheaper option at around $270 but Macaulay Library reports that it is noisier at high frequencies. With most birds and amphibians that probably is not an issue, but it is an issue with bats and insects, both of which will be covered in this guide.

It can be powered with AA size batteries.

Sennheiser ME 67/K6P

The Sennheiser ME 67 seems to be a favorite among bird recordists, but it requires the use of a battery pack.

B&H Photo sells a kit that has both for about $460. That kit is probably the way to go, the K6P battery pack is a good battery pack.

This is roughly a $200 price difference over the Rode NTG2.

For that price difference, I would get both better recordings of high audio frequencies and better directional recordings of everything, reducing the noises that I do not want to record when doing a Single Specimen Recording.

Pistol Grip with Foam Wind Screen

Regardless of which of the two shotgun microphones I end up getting—for recording wildlife audio—a pistol grip with a wind shield is an absolute must.

The directional mic will not be mounted to a video camera, and even the slightest breeze creates considerable noise in the recording if there is not a foam wind screen.

A proper pistol grip will have dampners to reduce vibration transmitted to the microphone from handling the grip.

The quality of the foam wind screen apparently can make a huge difference in recording quality if the wind is stronger than a gentle breeze.

There are some kits for both a pistol grip and a wind screen but I really need to do more research to make sure the kits contain quality for both.

The ‘Rycote Lyre Mount with Pistol Grip Handle’ looks like it probably is the grip to use, and it costs about $85.00. There is a kit that comes with that grip and what appears to be a decent wind screen for about $155.

Chorus Recording

For recording a chorus, whether it be frog or bird, I think I want to use a “Poor Man’s” binaural microphone.

One thing I have noticed with Sierran Chorus Frogs, when the entire pond is not in chorus, they often call in identifiable triplets.

Frog A will call from one part of the pond, followed by Frog B from a different part of the pond, and then Frog C from yet another.

I noticed a similar thing with Red-winged Blackbirds when at Cattail Pond at Helen Putnam Regional Park near Petaluma.

One group of two or three Red-winged blackbirds would call from one part of the pond, followed by a second elsewhere, followed by a third elsewhere, followed by silence. Then it would repeat.

A binaural microphone setup is basically a faux human head with realistic ears and a microphone in each ear. The intent is that by listening to the recording with headphones, you can get a three dimensional effect.

The expensive part of a binaural microphone is the fake head, they cost over $400 and I suspect are overkill for this project. I personally want to try using a seven inch diameter styrofoam ball. That is the approximate diameter of a human head. An eight inch styrofoam ball is about $10 or so at art supply stores and is close enough to seven inches.

The BE-P1 binaural microphone is $175. That includes a battery box needed for the amplifier.

With respect to the behavior I have observed in chorus frogs and blackbirds, my suspicion is that this done for the purpose of confusing predators that zoom in on their chorus. By calling in coordination from different parts of the pond, it may confuse some predators.

Do the frogs and blackbirds and frogs behave that way only when I am near because I am a perceived predator, thus an observer affecting the observed? Or do they also behave that way if I am some distance away?

I may never know the answer to that question, but I would like to try and record the behavior whether or not I can detrmine if it is in response to a potential threat stimuli.

If it works and the listener is wearing headphones, the stereo effect should be similar to if their head was where the binaural microphone was placed, and in theory they should be able to distinguish the relative direction different calls were coming from.

Ideally this would do this with the cooperation of a park ranger when the park is closed to the public to reduce the odds of device theft, but for recording things like Coyotes, it probably could be set up near a tent where I am camping without having an impact on the Coyote vocal behavior.

This binaural recording experiment is in the future, I will not be trying this for some time.
