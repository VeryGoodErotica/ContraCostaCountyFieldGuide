Field Guide Structure

There are several different types of content within this ePub book:

  1. Species Accounts
  2. Urban Trail Guides
  3. State and Regional Parks
  4. Educational Articles
  5. Appendix Content

This chapter attempts to explain how those types of content are organized.

Species Accounts

Species are broken up into what are informerly called ‘Taxon Groups’. I say informerly, because they will not always be monophyletic groupings, but will rather following the traditional groupings followed by print field guides. For example, field guides for Reptiles and Amphibians do not include Birds, making that grouping paraphyletic... (funny comic: https://xkcd.com/867/)

Each ‘Taxon Group’ within the field guide as a Taxon Curator and functions much like an independent field guide, only with the ability to link to species accounts in other ‘Taxon Groups’.

For example, if the Butterfly Taxon Curator is creating a species description for the Acmon Blue (Icaricia acmon), they may wish to link to the species profiles for the specific species of Buckwheats, Lupines, and Trefoils within the county that the caterpillars for these butterflies feed upon. They may also wish to wish to link to the types of ants that these caterpillars have a mutualistic relationship with.

If species profiles do not yet exist for the species the Butterfly Curator wishes to link to, at a minimum, the Taxon Curator for the needed species can create a stub entry that will allow the link to be created so that writing a species profile for that species becomes important.

The Butterfly curator may even wish to write the profile for the species outside their ‘Taxon Group’ if the curator for that Taxon has other priorities (or does not yet exist).

Taxon Sections

Within each ‘Taxon Group’, the curator for that group will split the group into logical sections, much like a field guide does.

In the case of vertebrate groups, it usually is probably best to use monophyletic taxonomical Orders for this splitting, but that will not always the case. Lizards make a logical grouping but actually is a paraphyletic grouping if it does not include snakes. Cormorants are technically in a different Order than Pelecaniformes but there are so few members of Suliformes in Contra Costa County that it makes sense to lump them with the Pelecaniformes as print field guides have traditionally done.

Each Taxon Section will have a page that acts as an index to all species within Contra Costa County for that section.

In addition to the index to each species, the Taxon Curator is encouraged to give additional information specific to the grouping.

When a Taxon Curator creates a section within their group, it is important that the curator attempt to list every species within the section even if there is no page for the species. This will help shutterbugs know what species need high quality photographs of wild specimens from within the county. It will also help naturalists who are not sure about an identification know what species to search for online if they at least are sure about the section the species probably falls into.

In many cases, even a section within a Taxon Group will have enough representatives within Contra Costa County that splitting up the section into related species makes sense.

How the Taxon Curator chooses to do this is largely at their discretion, but for an example of how I chose to do it with snakes, you can look at the local species on the Snakes page.
