California Red-legged Frog (Rana draytonii)

Species Overview

The California Red-legged Frog is our largest native frog, and while people say beauty is in the eye of the bee holder and I do not hold bees very often, I also believe it is our most beautiful frog. In the entire country, it is the most beautiful, with perhaps only the Oregon Spotted Frog (Rana pretiosa) as a legitimate co-contender for that designation.

Before the decline caused by overhavest in the gold rush era followed by the introduction of the American Bullfrog (Lithobates catesbeianus), the California Red-legged Frog was wide-spread through much of California and northern Baja California.

This species is one of two species of ‘True Frog’ that would have been found in the vicinity of Angel’s Camp in Calavaras County and is the only of of those two species that fits the story that jump-started (pun intended) Mark Twain’s career with The Celebrated Jumping Frog of Calavaras County.

Species Description

Despite being our largest native frog, the California Red-legged Frog is a medium-sized frog that rareley exceeds 13cm SNV (Snout to Vent) (just over 5in) with females reaching larger sizes than males. Females do not reach sexual maturity until they are about 8.5cm SNV at three years of age, while males reach sexual maturity at a smaller size at about two years of ageON2.

The base color of their back is typically a light brown to vibrant red with all shades between. Occassionally the base color is a shade of green. This species has very prominant dorsolateral folds along the sides of the body usually, with several distinct dark (sometimes black) spots on the back between the dorsolateral folds.

The spots are absent in some individuals. Some field guides describe the species as having light centers in the spotting but I have found that to be a farely inconsistent characteristic. From four different sites within Contra Costa County and one site in Marin county, my personal experience is the spotting is far more noticeable on young frogs than on adults although they are often still very prominant on many adults. While searching for remnant populations of Rana boylii in streams on Mount Diablo and surrounding areas, small frogs jumping into streams so far has always led to mixed emotions of dissapointment as I notice the spots and enthusiasm at having at least found my favorite frog, even if not the species I was hoping to find.

Their back and side skin is very granular with bumps, the bumps often seem to line up with the light centers in the spotting when light centers are present.

On individuals with a more brown or green base color, the dorsolateral fold often has a red or yellow tinge to it, making it extremely noticeable, but that is not always the case.

Very often on the side of the head, there is a white stripe running along the upper jaw line (sometimes referred to as a ‘jaw stripe’) starting somewhere between the eye and the nostril and ending just above the shoulder. Again, this morphological feature is not always present.

Reproduction

Reproduction takes place from late winter through early spring. The microhabit being used for reproduction plays a factor, with observation in Contra Costa County showing that egg masses were laid a month earlier in lentic habitat than in lotic habitatON1.

Egg masses are laid in shallow water and is usually attached to vegetation. When Cattail (Typha spp.) is available (typically lentic habitat) that seems to be the preferred choice, though other types of vegetation are also often utilized, especially in lotic habitats where Cattail is scarce.

An egg mass typically contains N–M eggs[] and are laid in shallow water, generally attacked to Cattail (Typha spp.) in lentic habitats thought other plants are also also used and a wide variety of plants are used in lotic habitats where Cattail is often not availableON1.

Reproduction generally takes place from mid-February to mid-March although it has been recorded as early as December and as late as Aprik [citation]

Males require a minimum of two years to reach sexual maturity while females require a minimum of three years.

Taxonomy Notes

The California Red-legged Frog was originally described by Baird & Girard as the subspecies Rana aurora draytonii in 1852.

Since then, the California Red-legged frog has been elevated to full species status. Interestingly, it appears that the Northern Red-legged Frog (Rana aurora) that the California Red-legged Frog formerly was described as a subspecies of, actually forms a clade with the Cascades Frog (Rana cascadae) and the Mountain Yellow-legged Frog complex (Rana muscosa) to the exclusion of the California Red-legged FrogON3.

The taxonomy within the Ranidae family is currently within a state of flux as we figure out evolutionary relationships.

Formerly, all members of the family within the United States fell into the genus Rana. Within North America, the species group sometimes referred to as the Rana boylii group (also referred to as the subgenus Amerana) have a more recent common ancestor with European and Asian frogs of the genus Rana than they do with other North American frogs in the Ranidae family. They are probably a newer arrival to the New World, perhaps having crossed the Bering Land Bridge from Asia at about the same the ancestor of the European Cave Salamanders (genus Speleomantes) and the European Pond Turtle (Emys orbicularis) were crossing over to Asia from North America.

Currently there are eight defined species within Amerana however it is quite possible that the Cascades Frog (Rana cascadae) and the Foothill Yellow-legged Frog (Rana boylii) are cryptic species complexes each containing more than one species.
