Painted Turtle (Chrysemys picta)

Species Overview

Stub. Very incomplete.

Painted Turtles are the second most commonly kept pet aquatic turtle species, second only to the Red-eared Slider (Trachemys scripta elegans).

They are not released nearly as often as Red-eared Sliders, but they are released often enough that there are several records for them in Contra Costa County within the iNaturalist database.

Most Painted Turtles in the pet trade are the Western subspecies (C. p. bellii) but the Eastern subspecies (C. p. picta) is also frequently available.

Distribution Range

Within Contra Costa County, this species is a Released Curiosity without any evidence of reproduction. It is most likely to be found in urban bodies of water, such as the artificial pond at Los Medanos College.

This species is not native to California.

Taxonomy Notes

The Painted Turtle was first described by Johann G. T. Schneider in 1783 as Testudo picta.

The Painted Turtle was moved out of Testudo into its own monotypic genus Chrysemys in 1855 by John Edward Gray. Unless you consider the Southern Painted Turtle to be a distinct species rather than a subspecies, the Painted Turtle is the only extant species in that genus.

The Southern Painted Turtle apparently does hybridize with both the Eastern and Midland subspecies where they come in contact and for that reason, many still consider it to be a subspecies. However, hybridization can occur while there still is a significant barrier to gene flow between the populations, so hybridization does not indicate the populations are on the same evolutionary path.

Subspecies

There are currently four recognized subspecies. These subspecies descriptions are based upon morphological characterists which as a general rule often vary significantly within a subspecies. Also note that the allele frequency of a population has constant natural selection pressures and the phenotype of a population does not always align with genetic clades.

With released Painted Turtles, you can often make a very good guess about subspecies that will often be correct, but when the specimen is not found within a well-known range, you can not always be sure.

Western Painted Turtle (C. p. bellii)

This is the subspecies most commonly sold in pet stores and thus released when owners no longer can care for them.

Western Painted Turtles usually have a vibrant red border on their plastron with a very detailed pattern within that border.

Even though the plastron is hard to see unless holding the turtle, the vibrant red can often be visually seen when they are basking. This characteristic is unique to this subspecies.

Eastern Painted Turtle (C. p. picta)

This subspecies is sometimes sold in pet stores, although not nearly as often as the Western Painted Turtle.

A rather distinctive feature of this subspecies, the vertebrate scutes usually line up with the adjacent costal scutes so that there are near perfect horizontal lines between scutes from the left marginal scutes to the right marginal scutes. This phenoma does not always happen, but it does with the majority of turtles of this subspecies. It does not happen, or very rarely happens, in other most other turtles, including other subspecies of the Painted Turtle.

The plastron is generally plain, sometimes with faint spots along the long axis.

As of February 2020, there is only one entry for this subspecies in the iNaturalist database within Contra Costa County: iNat 11305716. Other sightings are occassionally reported among field herpers.

Southern Painted Turtle (C. p. dorsalis)

This subspecies is not common in the pet trade but it does sometimes turn up there.

This subspecies usually has a very prominant red stripe running lengthwise down the carapace. This subspecies may actually be a distinct species in its own right rather than a subspecies of Chrysemys picta [Starkey et. al. 2003].

Midland Painted Turtle (C. p. marginata)

This subspecies is not common in the pet trade but it does sometimes turn up there.

If you are sure a turtle is a Painted Turtle and you have ruled out Western and Eastern and the turtle does not have a prominent red stripe down the carapace, it could be this subspecies.
